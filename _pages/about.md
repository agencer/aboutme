I am a Ph.D. candidate in Political Science at New York University. My research studies how citizens make political judgments when democratic threats are difficult to see, costly to evaluate, or embedded inside broader political choices.

My dissertation develops a behavioral account of democratic backsliding. I ask why citizens sometimes accept reforms, leaders, or political packages that contain harmful institutional consequences. The core idea is simple: democratic erosion often does not arrive as a clean, isolated choice. It arrives bundled with popular policies, framed through selective information, filtered through stereotypes, or justified through crisis management. These settings make accountability harder because citizens must first reconstruct what is at stake before they can evaluate it.

This research has been supported by the American Political Science Association Doctoral Dissertation Research Improvement Grant for **Unbundling Autocratic Capture**, one of the discipline’s major competitive grants for dissertation research. My related work has also received support from the Identities & Ideologies Project at NYU and the NYU George Downs Prize for dissertation research.

My work brings together comparative politics, political behavior, causal inference, survey experiments, and formal theory. Across my projects, I study four connected problems.

First, in **Unbundling Autocratic Capture**, I examine how complexity and selective presentation shape support for reform bundles with harmful institutional implications. The project tests whether citizens are less likely to detect anti-democratic provisions when those provisions are embedded inside larger, more attractive, or more cognitively demanding packages of policy change.

Second, in my work on **out-group stereotypes and affective polarization**, I study how citizens form beliefs about political opponents in backsliding democracies. This project develops tools for identifying stereotype-prone issues and tests whether correcting misperceptions about out-groups can reduce perceived extremity and affective hostility.

Third, in **Optimal Allocation of Compliance Incentives**, I study how researchers should design experiments when incentives affect treatment take-up, compliance, and cost. This project connects experimental design, causal inference, and decision theory by asking how limited research budgets should be allocated when compliance is heterogeneous and uncertain.

Fourth, in my crisis management work, I study how citizens evaluate incumbents after major disasters and public crises. These projects examine how crisis response, public investment, local exposure, and electoral accountability interact in ways that reward or punish different parts of the governing coalition.

More broadly, my research asks how democratic accountability works when citizens face complex information environments. I am especially interested in cases where political choices are not obviously authoritarian or democratic on the surface, but require citizens to notice, process, and weigh institutional consequences hidden inside broader political narratives.
